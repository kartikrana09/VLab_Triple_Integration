Aim

Learning Objectives

This virtual lab aims to provide students with a comprehensive understanding of the evaluation of triple integrals over bounded three-dimensional solid regions. By the end of this experiment, you will be able to:

  • Understand the geometric and physical interpretation of a triple integral.
  • Learn how to formulate integration limits for Cartesian, Cylindrical, and Spherical coordinate systems.
  • Choose the most appropriate coordinate system for a given solid region (e.g. rectangular box, cylinder, sphere, paraboloid, or tetrahedron) to simplify limits of integration.
  • Visualize the 3D solid region of integration and understand how integrals are evaluated through sequential "slicing" and "accumulation" sweeps.
  • Apply triple integration to calculate physical quantities such as volume, mass, and moments of inertia.

Experiment Overview

In multivariable calculus, triple integration is the extension of double integration to three dimensions. While double integration represents accumulating density over a 2D surface (finding area or mass of a lamina), triple integration accumulates density over a 3D solid (finding volume, total mass of a solid, centroids, or moments of inertia).

This lab allows you to interactively adjust the shape parameters of various 3D solids, swap between coordinate systems to see how limits change, and sweep slicing planes along the coordinate axes to visually grasp how three-dimensional integration sweeps accumulate volume.

Theory

Triple Integrals definition

Let \(f(x, y, z)\) be a continuous function defined on a closed, bounded solid region \(V\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\). The triple integral of \(f\) over the region \(V\) is defined as :

$$\iiint_V f(x, y, z) \, dV$$

Where \(dV\) is the infinitesimal volume element. The evaluation of a triple integral is performed by setting up iterated integrals in one of three coordinate systems.

1. Cartesian Coordinate System (x, y, z)

In Cartesian coordinates, the volume element is \(dV = dz \, dy \, dx\) (or any of the 6 permutations of the order of variables). When the region \(V\) is bounded by \(x \in [a, b]\), \(y \in [g_1(x), g_2(x)]\), and \(z \in [h_1(x,y), h_2(x,y)]\), we calculate the iterated integral as:

$$\iiint_V f(x, y, z) \, dV = \int_{a}^{b} \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} \int_{h_1(x,y)}^{h_2(x,y)} f(x,y,z) \, dz \, dy \, dx$$

2. Spherical Coordinate System (ρ, φ, θ)

Used when the region has radial symmetry about the origin (spheres, hemispheres, or cones). The transformations are:

$$x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta, \quad y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta, \quad z = \rho\cos\phi$$

Common Regions in Spherical Coordinates

The Jacobian determinant of transformation is \(J = \rho^2 \sin\phi\). The volume element is \(dV = \rho^2 \sin\phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta\), and the integral is:

$$\iiint_V f(x,y,z) \, dV = \int_{\theta_1}^{\theta_2} \int_{\phi_1}^{\phi_2} \int_{\rho_1(\phi, \theta)}^{\rho_2(\phi, \theta)} f(\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta, \dots) \cdot \rho^2 \sin\phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta$$

Physical Applications

Quantity Integrand \(f(x,y,z)\) Integral Formulation
Volume of Solid \(1\) \(V = \iiint_V dV\)
Total Mass Density \(\delta(x,y,z)\) \(M = \iiint_V \delta(x,y,z) \, dV\)
Centroid (\(\bar{z}\)) \(z \cdot \delta(x,y,z)\) \(\bar{z} = \frac{1}{M} \iiint_V z \cdot \delta(x,y,z) \, dV\)
Moment of Inertia (\(I_z\)) Distance squared: \(x^2 + y^2\) \(I_z = \iiint_V (x^2+y^2)\delta(x,y,z) \, dV\)
